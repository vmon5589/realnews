Federal Panel Warns Frontier AI Models Outpacing Safety Frameworks

A bipartisan commission urged a 90-day pre-deployment review for systems exceeding new compute thresholds, citing risks no current law fully addresses.

By Marisol Chen  Technology Reporter April 23, 2026 · 6:42 a.m. PT Share SaveComment

A federal advisory commission released a report Tuesday warning that the largest AI systems now in development outpace existing safety and disclosure frameworks, and recommended a mandatory 90-day pre-deployment review for any model trained above a new compute threshold. Bay Area researchers featured prominently among the 38 experts who contributed to the document.

The 214-page report does not propose halting development. It does call for a tiered system in which the most capable models — defined by training compute, not parameter count — would be subject to independent red-team evaluation, mandatory incident reporting, and a public summary of pre-deployment test results.

Industry response was mixed. Three of the largest model developers said they already follow comparable internal practices and would support codifying them. A coalition of smaller open-weight projects warned that the threshold, if set too low, could entrench incumbents and hamper academic research.

“The gap between capability and assurance is widening, and we don’t have years to close it.” — Dr. Yusuf Aydin, AI Risk Commission

What the rules would cover

The proposed framework would require pre-deployment evaluations on a defined set of catastrophic-misuse benchmarks, with results reviewed by a small federal staff and a rotating panel of outside auditors. Companies would not be required to disclose model weights, but would have to share evaluation methodology and results with regulators.

The commission stopped short of recommending a licensing regime, which several members called premature. Instead, it proposed beginning with notification and review, with possible tightening if specific risk indicators emerge during the first review cycle.

Bay Area lawmakers said they expect a draft bill to circulate within weeks, and that early conversations with state officials in Sacramento are also underway over a possible parallel California framework.
