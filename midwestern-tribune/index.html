Rural Hospital Closures Push Maternal-Care Coverage Gaps Wider

A statewide review counts 23 counties without a labor-and-delivery unit, up from 17 four years ago, and finds the steepest declines in the program areas with the longest drive times.

By Daniel Ostrowski  Health Reporter April 21, 2026 · 5:00 a.m. CT Share SaveComment

The state department of health released a review Wednesday showing that 23 counties no longer have a labor-and-delivery unit within their borders, up from 17 in the comparable 2022 report. The trend is steepest in the western and northern regions of the state, where average drive times to the nearest delivery hospital now exceed 45 minutes for more than a third of expecting parents.

The review attributes most of the closures to a combination of low birth volume at small-county hospitals, rising malpractice insurance costs for obstetric services, and a persistent shortage of OB/GYN clinicians outside metropolitan areas. Two of the closures in the past 18 months involved the discontinuation of obstetric services even as the rest of the hospital remained open.

Public-health researchers say the longer drive times track with measurable downstream effects: higher rates of preterm delivery, more emergency-department-only deliveries, and a meaningful uptick in postpartum readmission. The report stops short of attributing those outcomes to closures alone, but flags the correlation as “robust enough to act on.”

“A 50-mile drive in active labor is not a workable plan. We are pretending it is, and the data show what that pretending costs.” — Dr. Karen Boltz, State Maternal Health Initiative

What’s on the table

The legislature is reviewing a proposal that would create a graduated rural-OB designation, allowing smaller hospitals to maintain limited delivery services with supplemental funding for malpractice insurance and clinician recruitment. A separate piece of legislation would expand a mobile-clinic program currently piloted in two counties.

Hospital systems said they support both proposals in principle, but cautioned that designation alone will not solve the underlying clinician-shortage problem. The state medical school’s expansion of its rural-residency track, in its third year, is graduating its first cohort this spring.

The full report, including county-level maps, was published on the department’s public-records portal alongside the underlying machine-readable data.
