Arctic Heatwave Drives Greenland Ice-Sheet Melt to Record Pace

Spring temperatures across the polar north are running roughly four degrees Celsius above the long-term average, accelerating runoff and unsettling regional climate models.

By Niamh Calloway  Climate Correspondent April 22, 2026 · 7:10 a.m. PT Share SaveComment

The Greenland ice sheet is shedding mass at the fastest April pace on record, according to a joint readout from the Danish Meteorological Institute and a U.S.-led research consortium that includes scientists from three Pacific Northwest universities. Surface melt extent crossed 30 percent of the ice sheet earlier this week, weeks ahead of the typical seasonal threshold.

The cause, researchers said, is a persistent ridge of warm air parked over the Arctic for the better part of three weeks. Spring temperatures across the polar north are running roughly 4°C above the 1991-2020 average, with several stations on Svalbard recording multi-day stretches more than 10°C above their seasonal norms.

Ocean modelers said the runoff is also large enough to influence salinity and circulation patterns in the North Atlantic over a multi-year horizon. None of the immediate readings, they cautioned, point to a specific tipping-point threshold — but the cumulative trend is consistent with the upper bound of recent forecasts.

“We are no longer surprised by anomalies of this size. We are increasingly worried that they are becoming the baseline.” — Dr. Asha Mendelson, Pacific Climate Center

Downstream effects

Coastal planning agencies on the West Coast said the new figures will be folded into the next round of sea-level rise projections, which are due to be revised next year. Several local jurisdictions said they expect to advance their planning timelines on shoreline-protection projects as a result.

Researchers stressed that a single melt season does not redefine long-term forecasts on its own. But the third consecutive year of significantly above-average melt, they said, is now hard to reconcile with the lower-emission climate scenarios that informed earlier infrastructure decisions.
